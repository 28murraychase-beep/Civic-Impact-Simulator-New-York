Current Limitations

This version of CivicImpact uses transparent, documented assumptions; simplifies complex policy interactions into single-equation models; and is intended for educational and exploratory use. It does not replace official fiscal scoring (e.g. a CBO or state fiscal office estimate) and should not be interpreted as an official government forecast.

Planned improvements include uncertainty/Monte Carlo analysis, empirical calibration against real program outcomes, and additional datasets — see the roadmap below.

Featured Case Study

Could New York's proposed $335 million housing bond instead finance first-time homebuyer assistance?

Purpose

This case study walks through one concrete, real-dollar-figure question end to end — not as a hypothetical, but as a worked example of how every other scenario in this app gets built — so a reader can see exactly how an input becomes an output, and where the uncertainty actually lives.

Background

New York State and New York City periodically reallocate or refinance municipal bond proceeds across program areas. A reallocation of this size — $335 million — is large enough to fund a meaningful down-payment assistance program, but the question of whether it should go there instead of its original purpose is exactly the kind of tradeoff this simulator is built to make visible, not settle.

Model Assumptions

Full table with sources and confidence levels is rendered live below once you run the scenario — see Simulation Outputs. In short: the budget, per-recipient amount, and home-price figures are direct, chosen inputs (High confidence); the leverage ratio is mechanical arithmetic (High confidence); how many recipients actually complete a purchase, and what happens to home prices on the demand side, are not modeled here and are flagged explicitly in Limitations below.

Economic Tradeoffs

Demand-side assistance without a matching increase in housing supply tends to bid up prices in the same market it's trying to make affordable — the single biggest tension in this scenario. Pairing this with the Housing Supply & Zoning Reform model in the National Policy Library would show the net affordability effect more honestly than either model alone. Because this is funded by reallocation rather than new revenue, the real cost isn't the $67M/yr shown — it's the opportunity cost of whatever the bond proceeds were originally going to fund, which this model does not quantify.

Political Tradeoffs

This scenario scores a polarization index of roughly 52/100 — high messaging risk. Progressives broadly favor direct, fast-acting aid but worry it does nothing for underlying supply constraints. Conservatives are open to targeted, credit-score-gated cash aid framed around self-reliance, but watch costs and fraud/eligibility risk closely. Libertarians generally prefer cash over in-kind programs but still oppose the underlying reallocation itself. See the full breakdown, including which voter coalitions in your selected county actually lean which way, in the Build Your Own Scenario and NY County tabs.

Limitations

  • Does not model how many "families reachable" actually complete a home purchase versus simply receiving an offer of assistance.
  • Does not model the price-pressure feedback loop on the homes being purchased.
  • Treats the $450,000 average purchase price as constant statewide, when real prices vary by more than 10× across NY counties — see the NY County Housing Explorer for a county-specific version of this same scenario.
  • Does not quantify the opportunity cost of the reallocated bond proceeds' original purpose.

Future Research

The Roadmap (bottom of every page) lists Monte Carlo uncertainty ranges and historical validation as the two highest-priority additions that would most improve a case study like this one — replacing single-point estimates like "22,333 families" with a defensible range, and checking that range against how similar real programs (SONYMA, NYC HomeFirst) actually performed.

How this works

The Policy Library runs 15 nationally-calibrated models, each with its own tuned assumptions. The Scenario Builder works differently: almost any real program — anywhere in the world — reduces to a mechanism (what the money does), a funding source (where the money comes from), and a population/budget. Pick those three, and the same transparent math that powers the Library runs your custom idea. That's how one engine can model your city's $335M bond reallocation and a national paid-leave bill without needing a separate hand-built entry for each.

This is the browser-based companion to a full Python/Colab notebook with sensitivity analysis and exportable memos. Every number is a directional estimate from documented assumptions, not a forecast — change any input and the ledger updates instantly so you can see exactly which assumptions are driving the result.

Roadmap

Upcoming improvements, roughly in priority order:

  • Monte Carlo / uncertainty ranges instead of single-point estimates
  • Confidence intervals shown directly on every chart
  • Historical validation against real program outcomes where data exists
  • County-level forecasting for states beyond New York
  • Live API integration (Census/BLS/FRED) instead of static calibration
  • Exportable PDF policy reports
  • Downloadable policy memos (Markdown/Word)

About the Creator

CivicImpact was created by Chase Murray, a student and New York native passionate about political science, economics, and civic technology.

The project began as a Google Colab research notebook exploring how interactive simulations could make public policy analysis more transparent and accessible. It has since evolved into a browser-based platform that helps users examine fiscal, economic, demographic, and political tradeoffs under clearly documented assumptions.

CivicImpact is an independent educational project and is not affiliated with any government agency, political campaign, or advocacy organization.
